## Situation

Red's forces held individually superior units (titans vs. survey corps) and methodically eliminated three of Blue's weaker units across the first four turns. By turn 4, Red appeared to be winning the attrition war, controlling more health and killing power across the board.

## Lesson

Blue's winning insight was recognizing that **unit preservation and objective completion are separate problems**—and only the latter matters.

Blue implemented a two-part strategy:

1. **Concentrated defensive effort only on the win condition**: Eren Titan and its critical support (Mikasa for threat elimination, Medic for healing). Survey corps units were written off as acceptable losses.

2. **Maintained objective momentum while Red fragmented**: When Mikasa achieved a decisive 18-damage kill with zero counter damage (turn 2), it proved Red's forces lacked unified positioning. Red spent turns 3–8 chasing scattered threats across the map while Eren advanced methodically toward the winning tile.

3. **Won decisively early**: Eren reached the objective on turn 9, with 7 turns remaining. Red never consolidated to block.

Red's strategic failure: treating all units as equally defensible. Every unit death triggered a reaction—but Red never asked whether defending *that unit* mattered more than stopping the Eren Titan. Blue asked that question and chose tempo over attrition.

**Principle**: In objective-based games, defend the objective and its enablers; trade everything else for board position and tempo. The attrition war is irrelevant if you've already won.
