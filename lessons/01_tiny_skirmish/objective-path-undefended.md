## Situation

Blue won by seizing the objective at (5, 5) on turn 3. Analysis of unit positioning reveals Red failed to contest or defend the seizure point despite having clear visibility of Blue's advance.

Blue's archer moved systematically toward the right edge of the map: (5, 0) → (5, 4) → (5, 5). This patrol was never interrupted. Meanwhile, Red's archer inexplicably moved to the left edge (0, 5) → (0, 1), placing it furthest possible from the seizure point. Red's knight reached (4, 4) by turn 3, but was only one square away—too late to contest or block.

The critical failure: Red never positioned any unit to deny Blue access to the objective.

## Lesson

In seize-based victory conditions, the objective location is not an emergency discovered at turn 3—it is the governing constraint of the match from turn 1. **Identify the target early and dedicate at least one unit to contesting or controlling that zone.** Red scattered forces reactively instead of strategically.

Generalizable principle: **Map control beats damage trading.** Red landed a 5-damage hit on Blue's archer but failed to defend the one square that mattered. In asymmetric objectives, positional dominance over attrition determines the outcome. Every unit placement should answer: "Does this help hold or deny the objective?" Red's archer at (0, 1) answered "no."
