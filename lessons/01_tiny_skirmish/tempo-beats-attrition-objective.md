## Situation

Blue won by seizing the objective at (5,5) while Red was still engaged in killing Blue's archer. Blue's archer retreated progressively across the map (5,0 → 1,0 → 0,0), drawing both of Red's units into a chase sequence that lasted three full turns. Red committed substantial movement and actions to hunting it down. Meanwhile, Blue's knight advanced toward the seizure point largely uncontested (3,1 → 5,2 → 5,5). On turn 4, the knight walked into the seizure zone and won.

## Lesson

Red pursued an **attrition strategy**—eliminate threats, control the map—against an opponent executing a **tempo strategy** where speed to the objective trumps unit survival. By accepting the archer's eventual death and using it as a delaying distraction, Blue traded one unit's survival for the other's uncontested path to victory.

The principle: **When the win condition is time-dependent (seizure, escort, survival), tempo beats attrition.** Resources spent destroying non-blocking threats are wasted if the opponent completes their objective faster. Red should have either raced the knight to the seizure zone or consolidated to defend it—not chased the archer across the board.

This applies broadly: the player with a unit suited to the win condition should *accept combat losses* to enable that unit's success. The opponent must never let themselves be drawn into fights that don't deny the objective itself. Red's mistake was confusing "kill enemy units" with "prevent the enemy from winning."
