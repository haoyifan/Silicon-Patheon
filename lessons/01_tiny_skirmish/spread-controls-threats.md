## Situation

Red won by seizing the objective at (0,0) on turn 3. The deciding factor was **positional geometry**: Red's archer started at (0,5)—directly aligned with the seize point—while Blue's units clustered on the right side of the board (archer at 5,0, knight at 2,0). When Red moved the archer down the left edge via (0,1) to (0,0), Blue's units were too far away to contest the objective or even zone control it.

Blue's knight attempted a late-game counter-push toward (0,0), reaching (1,1), but arrived one turn too late. By turn 4, Red's archer had already seized the point.

## Lesson

**Early unit spreading forces opponents into a defensive dilemma.** By deploying to opposite corners (archer at 0,5, knight at 5,5), Red created two separate threat axes that Blue could not defend simultaneously:

1. **Objective ambiguity**: Blue had to guess which side of the map held the critical seize point. With units spread, Red could pivot toward any objective without repositioning.

2. **Forced overcommitment**: Blue's concentration of both units on the right meant zero coverage of the left flank. This isn't a mistake in isolation—it's a consequence of facing multiple threats.

3. **Efficient lines**: The archer's path (0,5 → 0,1 → 0,0) exploited a clear corridor. Blue's scattered positioning meant no unit could intercept or deny this path.

**Generalizable principle**: On larger maps where units cannot immediately dominate all territory, spatial distribution early in the game creates asymmetric pressure. The opponent must either:
- Spread defensively (weak everywhere), or  
- Concentrate offensively (weak somewhere)

Red exploited the second horn by racing to an undefended objective while Blue committed to the right side. The win came not from superior combat stats, but from better **positional initiative**—moving first into space Blue couldn't cover.
