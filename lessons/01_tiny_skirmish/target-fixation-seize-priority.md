## Situation

Red engaged blue's archer repeatedly across turns 2–4, dealing cumulative damage (6+5 on turn 2; 6 on turn 4) while blue's damaged archer retreated westward. During this same window, blue's knight—initially at (3,1)—pivoted toward the seize objective at (5,5), reaching it uncontested on turn 4, turn 20 of the game's maximum 20.

Red killed blue's archer on turn 4 but *after* blue had already won. Red's units were positioned offensively (knight at (1,0), archer at (4,0)) and never moved to interdict blue's knight's northeastward escape route.

## Lesson

In objective-centric scenarios (seize, hold, escort), **successful damage output does not equal victory**. Red's archer and knight executed good tactical exchanges—landing hits and avoiding lethal counter-damage—but this created a false sense of progress.

Blue's knight was never in real danger of death; once damaged units disengage and move *away* from you, pursuing the kill becomes a distraction. Red should have recognized on turn 3 (when blue's knight moved to (5,2)) that the opponent was repositioning for the objective and pivoted its own units to either:

1. **Contest the objective directly** – Move knights toward (5,5) to block or delay seize
2. **Cut movement lanes** – Position archers to threaten the path, forcing blue to choose between speed and safety
3. **Accept the loss trade-off** – If units can't reach in time, accept a tactical loss to minimize damage

The game's tight turn economy (20 total) made every movement decision critical. Three red turns spent on a single enemy unit collapsed when one blue unit reached an undefended goal.

**Pattern**: Don't let a winning engagement feel like strategic control. In timed or objective modes, measure success by *proximity to win conditions*, not kill count.
