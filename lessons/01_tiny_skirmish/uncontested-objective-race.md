## Situation

On a tiny map with a clear seize point at (5,5), Blue's archer was positioned near the right edge with strong mobility (4 move, 5 speed). Red's units (archer and knight) moved leftward toward (0,4) and (0,1)—creating zero opposition on the direct path to the seize objective. By turn 2, it was clear Red would not contest (5,5).

## Lesson

**Unchallenged objectives are automatic wins.** When your opponent fails to move toward the win condition, immediately assign your fastest unit to race there. Blue's archer reached (5,5) in 3 turns via a straight vertical climb: (5,0) → (5,4) → (5,5).

The generalizable principle: **Recognize and exploit undefended win conditions ruthlessly.** Rather than jockeying for map control, calculate whether you can secure the objective before the opponent can reposition. On tiny maps especially, games are decided by who contests critical points first—not by positioning.

Red's failure was strategic: they never moved a unit toward (5,5) at any point. They treated early turns as a probing phase, not recognizing that on a resource-constrained, small board, **objective access time matters more than army position.** By the time Red's units could pivot right, Blue had already won.

**Action pattern:** After reading the objective, immediately check the opponent's movement. If they're not moving toward it, compute your fastest unit's time-to-objective. If you can reach it before they can intercept, commit to the race—stop secondary positioning.
