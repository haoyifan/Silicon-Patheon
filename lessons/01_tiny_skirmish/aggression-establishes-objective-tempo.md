## Situation

On a tiny skirmish map with a seize objective at (0,0), Red's archer and knight faced Blue's paired units. Turn 1 saw Red's archer move aggressively into range and attack Blue's archer, dealing mutual damage (6-6) but establishing momentum. This early aggression forced Blue into a reactive posture: their units spent subsequent turns managing the wounded archer's position and attempting counter-trades rather than contesting the objective.

Meanwhile, Red's knight methodically moved across the map toward (0,0). By turn 3, with Blue's units scattered and focused on their damaged archer, Red's knight reached the objective unopposed and seized victory.

## Lesson

**Controlled aggression early in a seize match can establish tempo, forcing enemies into reaction cycles while you claim the objective.**

The critical insight: Red's initial archer attack wasn't meant to win through combat—it was a *positioning tool*. By damaging Blue's archer and threatening further engagement, Red forced Blue to allocate mental and positional bandwidth to that duel. Blue's knight tried to support the archer; Blue's archer retreated and repositioned. These are natural defensive reactions, but they consumed turns and movement.

Meanwhile, Red disengaged the archer engagement and committed the knight to an objective run. With Blue's units locked in a damage-mitigation cycle at the center of the map, no unit could reach (0,0) in time to intercept.

**The generalizable principle:** In objective-based scenarios, opening aggression that damages (not necessarily kills) an opponent's key units can establish initiative. The opponent must choose between:
- Ignoring the threat (bleeding resources)
- Responding to it (burning turns on non-objective actions)

Whichever they choose, you gain a tempo window. Use that window to execute your primary objective while defenders are distracted or repositioning.

This applies even in longer matches: early damage that limits an opponent's options—forcing them to heal, retreat, or cluster defensively—can ripple into tempo advantages that favor objective execution over pitched battles. The goal isn't to fight; it's to create asymmetry in what both sides are thinking about.
