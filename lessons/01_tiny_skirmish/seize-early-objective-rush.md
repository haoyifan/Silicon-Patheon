## Situation

Blue won by seizing objective at (5,5) on turn 3 with archer. Red's archer, which had move=4 (faster than Blue's), moved to (0,5) on turn 1, then retreated to (0,1) on turn 2—placing it *further* from the objective. By the time Red recognized the threat, Blue's archer was already at (5,5).

Red's moves suggest tactical confusion: the knight was positioned defensively at (2,4) and (0,4), but in a 3-turn loss, positional defense became irrelevant.

## Lesson

**In seize-to-win scenarios, the first mover to the objective wins—not the best-positioned army.** Red treated the scenario as a traditional skirmish, building a defensive line. Blue treated it as a race.

Generalizable principle: **Objective type dictates early game priority.**
- **Seize scenarios**: Send your fastest unit on a direct line to the objective immediately. This is a sprint, not a skirmish.
- **Defeat-all scenarios**: Positional strength and army composition matter more.
- **Survival scenarios**: Defensive positioning takes precedence.

Red's archer had the mobility advantage (move=4) but squandered it by:
1. Not rushing straight toward (5,5) from turn 1
2. Moving laterally/backward instead of toward the known objective
3. Treating the early turns as reconnaissance rather than commitment

In seize, you cannot afford to "feel out" the map. Identify the objective location early (if possible), and commit your fastest unit unconditionally. Losing a unit to an enemy while rushing is acceptable—losing the race is not.

The decision to move archer to (0,5) then (0,1) instead of pushing toward (5,5) was the turning point. Every turn of delay against a dedicated opponent in seize is a turn closer to defeat.
