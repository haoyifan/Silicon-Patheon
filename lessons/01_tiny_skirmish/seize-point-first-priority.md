## Situation
Blue engaged Red's forces with archer attacks (1 damage turn 2, 5 damage turn 3) while Red's archer advanced freely from (0,5) to the seize point at (0,0), securing victory on turn 4. Blue's units only began contesting the seize point after Red had already captured it on an uncontested final turn.

## Lesson
In seize-based objectives, the location of the goal must dictate unit positioning from turn one. Chasing tactical trades—damaging enemy units far from the objective—cedes strategic initiative to an opponent executing a focused advance. Red won by prioritizing the objective; Blue lost by pursuing combat effectiveness elsewhere.

Early turns should establish a presence at or near the goal, making future interception possible or contesting the advance. A single unit on or adjacent to the seize point by turn two would have forced Red to defend or engage differently, rather than marching unopposed. This principle generalizes: in objective-driven modes, proximity to the goal is strategically more valuable than damage output when both cannot be achieved simultaneously. Tempo toward the win condition always beats local tactical superiority.
