## Situation

Red achieved victory in 11 turns by eliminating Harry Potter (blue's VIP) through Dolohov's relentless pursuit across the board. Despite taking heavy counter-damage in mid-game exchanges (5 damage on turn 4), Red never wavered from positioning for continued attacks on the same target. Harry moved defensively but remained isolated, while Blue's reinforcements (Sirius, Moody, Lupin, Tonks) clustered around their other units rather than concentrating defense on the VIP.

## Lesson

**In VIP scenarios, single-target tunnel vision outperforms balanced/reactive play.**

Red's winning pattern was Dolohov repositioning every turn to maintain adjacency or attack range on Harry, regardless of map position or immediate threats. This forced Blue into a reactive posture—spreading their offense while trying to cover a scattered asset. Even when Dolohov took 5 counter-damage and dropped to low health, remaining committed to the hunt proved superior to pivoting to self-preservation or spreading pressure across multiple targets.

The principle generalizes: **objective-driven persistence forces opponents to defend a dispersed asset at cost**. When a defender must protect a mobile, isolated unit, the attacker who refuses to be distracted by baiting, counterpressure, or attrition will eventually isolate and eliminate it. Blue's mistake was not concentrating Harry's defense early—they invested in eliminating Red's units without recognizing that each failed defense of the VIP was a turn closer to loss.

Apply this when defending: colocate your VIP with your strongest defenders from turn one. Apply when attacking: commit your strongest hunter to the VIP and accept friendly casualties elsewhere.
