## Situation

By turn 2, Tang Monk (your VIP) had been isolated and killed in just 3 turns of play. Red executed a focused kill sequence: first concentrated fire on Bai Long Ma (your designated screen unit) to eliminate him, then used ranged attackers (White-Bone Demoness at 2–3 range) to delete Tang Monk before he could escape.

The formation collapse happened because Bai Long Ma was deployed at (7,4)—closer to enemy lines than Tang Monk at (4,4)—making him the natural focus target rather than a protective shield. While Sha Wujing tried to screen nearby, the primary defensive asset was isolated and overwhelmed, leaving the VIP defenseless.

## Lesson

**Defensive structure must precede aggressive positioning.** In asymmetric scenarios where one asset (the VIP) is a loss condition, your tank/screen units must be *between* the VIP and the enemy, not in front of them. 

The error was deploying for offense before establishing a viable defensive perimeter. Bai Long Ma should have stayed closer to Tang Monk (within 1–2 tiles) to genuinely block incoming attacks—especially from ranged casters—rather than pushing forward where he could be isolated and focus-fired.

Key principle: **Build a defensive core around your VIP first.** Tank units should form a shield line; only secondary/expendable units (scouts, skirmishers) advance aggressively. Reversing this—pushing your strongest defensive unit away from your VIP—invites exactly the scenario that unfolded: the enemy kills your screen, then executes the VIP freely.

Apply this whenever defending a critical asset: proximity matters more than forward momentum.
