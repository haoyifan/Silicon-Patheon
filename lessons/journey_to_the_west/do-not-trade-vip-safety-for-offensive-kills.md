## Situation

On *journey_to_the_west*, blue must escort Tang Monk (the VIP) across the map. Sun Wukong is blue's strongest unit by far (14 ATK, 5 move, 42 HP) and the only piece that can reliably one-round red's threats. Starting from turn 1, I sent Wukong east to duel Bull Demon (x=7) and then Demon King (x=3→2), racking up three solo kills across 6 turns. Meanwhile Tang Monk drifted along the western edge with only Bajie, Wujing, and Bai Long Ma as escorts.

Red's Spider Spirit 2 — a fast, fragile assassin (SPD 8, move 5) — was never prioritised. It slipped south, hit Tang Monk for 10 damage on turn 5, and no blue unit was positioned to finish it. On turn 6, I moved Tang Monk to (0,1), *closer* to the spider at (0,2), instead of retreating behind a screen. Wukong, fresh off killing Demon King at (2,2), was two tiles and a turn too far away. Spider Spirit 2 walked in and dealt the final 6 damage. Game over, turn 7 of 20.

## Lesson

**In VIP-protection scenarios, your strongest combat unit is a bodyguard first and a damage dealer second.**

Killing Bull Demon and Demon King felt productive — they were the biggest stat-blocks on the board. But neither was an immediate threat to Tang Monk. The actual threat was a 7-ATK spider that needed one clean hit. Wukong parked adjacent to Tang Monk would have deterred or instantly killed any approach; instead he was 5+ tiles away doing optional damage.

Generalizable rules:

- **Evaluate threats by proximity-to-VIP, not by raw stats.** A weak, fast unit near the VIP is more dangerous than a strong, slow unit far away.
- **Never leave the VIP's zone-of-control empty to chase kills.** If your best piece leaves, the opponent will punish the gap — that's the whole point of VIP-loss as a win condition.
- **After a VIP takes damage, the next action must address *that attacker*.** I let Spider Spirit 2 live after it dealt 10 damage to Tang Monk, then moved Tang Monk toward it. That sequence is indefensible.

Kills don't matter if the monk dies.
