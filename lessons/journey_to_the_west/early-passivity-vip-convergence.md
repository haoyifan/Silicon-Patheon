## Situation

Red won by eliminating Tang Monk on turn 4. Blue's turn 1 strategy was entirely passive: all units moved into a tight formation (x-range 3–7, y-range 3–6) around Tang Monk and then waited, never engaging Red's deployment. Red used this window to deploy freely across a wider formation (x-range 7–10), establishing spacing and sightlines. By turn 4, Red executed a perfectly coordinated multi-unit attack: White-Bone Demoness (14 dmg), Demon King (9 dmg to Bai Long Ma), Bull Demon (2 dmg to finish Bai Long Ma), and Skeleton 2 (2 dmg to finish Tang Monk). Blue never disrupted this sequence and Tang Monk fell.

## Lesson

In VIP-survival scenarios, **early passivity is a critical liability.** By waiting for Red to move first and consolidating defensively, Blue surrendered the initiative and allowed Red to achieve superior positioning and setup. Red faced zero pressure during deployment, which meant no tactical cost to their aggressive spacing and convergence prep.

The generalizable principle: **the team defending the VIP should establish harassment and board control early, not consolidate around the target.** Pushing fast units (Sun Wukong) forward to engage Red's deploying units would have forced Red into reactive positioning, consumed their action economy, and denied them the freedom to set up ideal focus-fire geometry. Early aggression limits enemy options, creates chaos in their setup phase, and often generates the breathing room needed for VIP survival. Passive defense invites the enemy to solve the puzzle of your formation without time pressure.
