## Situation

The objective was positional—reach the goal line (y=13) rather than destroy all enemy units. Red's army was geographically dispersed with multiple unit types available. Blue held a consolidated defensive position with ranged superiority (multiple longbowmen). Red's initial response in Turn 1 was to wait and consolidate, then sent French Knight 7 as the primary breakthrough vector while keeping most support units (men-at-arms, crossbows) at medium range and stationary.

## Lesson

The critical decision was treating the victory condition as *singular and positional* rather than *distributed and attritional*. Red ruthlessly concentrated forward momentum through one elite unit (Knight 7), which absorbed cumulative damage (15+ total from hostile actions) while pressing toward the goal line. Other units—particularly the knights sent forward in earlier turns—served implicitly as acceptable losses to occupy Blue's attention and damage output.

Knight 7 succeeded with only 6 HP remaining, demonstrating that positional breakthrough requires:

1. **Identifying which unit must reach the objective** and giving it exclusive priority
2. **Accepting high casualty rates in the vanguard** if they enable that unit's progress
3. **Using durable units** (high HP, armor) for breakthrough roles—lighter units crumple too quickly to soak sustained fire
4. **Ignoring peripheral losses**—units far from the objective are expendable; momentum matters more than preservation

This inverts traditional defensive doctrine: instead of trying to minimize overall casualties, concentrate all firepower and health into a narrow penetration point and sacrifice everything else. In positional objectives, one survivor reaching the goal is a complete victory; losing the entire army elsewhere is irrelevant.
