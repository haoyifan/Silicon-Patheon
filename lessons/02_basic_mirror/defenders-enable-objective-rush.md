## Situation

In this mirror match, both sides started with identical unit compositions: 2 knights, 1 archer, 1 cavalry, and 1 mage (Red had one). The map presented a clear strategic objective at (11, 11), isolated in the southeast corner.

Red chose to engage Blue's army frontally in the center-left region of the map. Blue's defending units (2 knights + archer) traded aggression: Blue's archer eliminated Red's cavalry early, then both sides maneuvered for position. Red consolidated all four remaining units into a tight cluster around (0–3, 6–8), clearly committed to a frontal assault.

Meanwhile, Blue's cavalry—the unit with the highest movement value (6 squares)—was sent on a parallel path eastward, advancing steadily toward the seize objective at (11, 11) while Red's entire force was occupied with Blue's infantry in the center.

## Lesson

**The winning pattern: Use a fixing force to concentrate enemy attention while a mobile unit achieves the objective.**

Red fell into a classic tactical trap. By committing all units to the center-map engagement, they:
- Allowed Blue's fastest unit an uncontested path to the objective
- Created no defensive presence at the far corner
- Left no unit with sufficient movement to intercept the cavalry in time

Blue's three slower units (knights + archer) acted as a *psychological anchor*—they forced Red into local combat mentality, making Red treat the fight as the primary objective. This is a generalizable principle:

1. **Identify the real objective** (often not the frontline engagement)
2. **Create a holding action** with units you can afford to keep engaged
3. **Route the mobile unit separately** to the objective, not through enemy lines
4. **Exploit opponent tunnel vision** on the visible conflict

In mirror matchups especially, the player who recognizes that *not all units need to fight* gains a decisive advantage. Red never asked: "Can we defend the objective?" They only asked: "How do we win the engagement?" By turn 7, Blue's cavalry reached the corner untouched.

Key takeaway: **Objectives often reward speed and positioning, not engagement.** Split your forces strategically—some to distract, some to deliver.
