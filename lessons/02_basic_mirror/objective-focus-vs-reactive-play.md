## Situation

In a mirror scenario with symmetrical forces, the victory condition was **seize**: reach and hold position (11,11). Red's cavalry was eliminated early (turn 1, by Blue's archer). In response, Red spent the next three turns consolidating on the left flank, cycling through moves and waits without committing toward the seize point. Meanwhile, Blue's cavalry methodically advanced toward (11,11), reaching and seizing it unopposed by turn 4.

Red finally began repositioning toward the objective on turn 5—two turns too late.

## Lesson

**In objective-based tactical games, a single unit loss should not disrupt your primary strategy.** 

Red's defeat stems from reactive rather than proactive play. Losing the cavalry triggered a defensive posture—consolidation, regrouping—that implicitly abandoned the seize race. This is a common trap: immediate threats (enemy positioning, units lost) feel urgent and demand a response, but they only matter if they prevent you from achieving the actual win condition.

Blue's cavalry had higher movement (6 vs Red's comparable units), and Blue recognized it could out-race Red to the objective despite the asymmetry. Red never noticed. By the time Red pivoted toward (11,11), Blue's cavalry was already there.

**The principle**: In any win-condition-focused scenario (seize, defend, survive-until-turn-X), the primary strategic decision tree must always include a path to that condition. Tactical reactions to threats are valid only when they don't subordinate your winning move to your opponent's damage output.

Red should have either: (a) raced Blue to the seize point from turn 1, or (b) committed to a defensive strategy that blocked Blue's path. Instead, Red did neither.
